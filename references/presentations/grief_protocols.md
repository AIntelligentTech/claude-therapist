# Grief Protocols Reference

> Comprehensive approaches to grief, loss, and bereavement support.

---

## Understanding Grief

### What Is Grief?

Grief is the natural response to loss. It encompasses the emotional, cognitive, physical, behavioral, social, and spiritual reactions to losing someone or something significant.

### Types of Loss

**Death-related**:
- Death of loved one
- Anticipatory grief (terminal illness)
- Sudden/traumatic death
- Suicide loss
- Pet loss

**Non-death losses**:
- Relationship ending (divorce, breakup)
- Loss of health/ability
- Job loss/career change
- Loss of home
- Loss of future (infertility, changed life plans)
- Loss of identity
- Loss of safety (after trauma)
- Loss of innocence

**All grief is valid. The magnitude of grief relates to the significance of the attachment, not objective measures of "importance."**

## Grief Theory Evolution

### What We Used to Believe
- Grief follows predictable stages
- There's a "right" way to grieve
- Goal is to "get over" the loss
- Should be resolved within a year
- Continuing bonds with deceased is unhealthy

### Current Understanding
- Grief is highly individual
- No universal timeline
- The relationship continues, transformed
- Grief oscillates, doesn't progress linearly
- Integration, not resolution

## Theoretical Models

### Worden's Four Tasks of Mourning

**Task 1: Accept the Reality of the Loss**
- Intellectual AND emotional acceptance
- Countering denial
- Loss is permanent
- Person is not coming back

**Task 2: Process the Pain of Grief**
- Feel the full range of emotions
- Not suppress or avoid
- Pain is necessary for healing
- Different people process differently

**Task 3: Adjust to a World Without the Deceased**
- External adjustments (new roles, skills)
- Internal adjustments (identity without them)
- Spiritual adjustments (meaning, worldview)
- Learning new ways of being in the world

**Task 4: Find an Enduring Connection While Embarking on New Life**
- Relocate the deceased emotionally
- Maintain bond while reinvesting in life
- Not "letting go" but finding new place for them
- "Carrying them with you" differently

### Dual Process Model (Stroebe & Schut)

Healthy grieving involves oscillation between:

**Loss-Oriented Coping**:
- Grief work
- Dwelling on the deceased
- Processing the loss
- Intrusion of grief
- Breaking bonds/relocating

**Restoration-Oriented Coping**:
- Attending to life changes
- Doing new things
- Distraction from grief
- New roles/identity
- Building new relationships

**Oscillation is normal and healthy**:
- Too much loss-focus: overwhelming, stuck
- Too much restoration-focus: avoidance
- Balance shifts over time

### Continuing Bonds

The relationship with the deceased continues in transformed form:
- Internal conversations
- Keeping possessions
- Rituals and traditions
- Sensing presence
- Carrying their values/lessons
- Anniversary observances

This is healthy, not pathological.

## Normal Grief Responses

### Emotional
- Sadness, sorrow
- Anger (at deceased, self, world, God)
- Guilt ("I should have...")
- Anxiety and fear
- Loneliness
- Yearning and longing
- Shock and disbelief
- Relief (especially after prolonged illness)
- Numbness

### Cognitive
- Disbelief
- Confusion
- Preoccupation with deceased
- Sense of presence
- Hallucinations (hearing/seeing them—common, not pathological)
- Difficulty concentrating

### Physical
- Fatigue and low energy
- Sleep disturbances
- Appetite changes
- Physical symptoms similar to deceased
- Weakened immunity
- Crying
- Sighing

### Behavioral
- Sleep disturbances
- Appetite changes
- Social withdrawal
- Dreams of deceased
- Avoiding reminders
- Searching behaviors
- Restlessness
- Overactivity or underactivity

## Complicated/Prolonged Grief

### DSM-5-TR: Prolonged Grief Disorder

**Criteria**:
- Death of close person at least 12 months ago (6 months for children)
- Persistent longing/yearning OR preoccupation with deceased
- Plus 3 or more of:
  - Identity disruption
  - Disbelief about death
  - Avoidance of reminders
  - Intense emotional pain
  - Difficulty moving on
  - Emotional numbness
  - Life feels meaningless
  - Intense loneliness
- Causes significant distress or impairment
- Duration and severity beyond cultural/religious norms

### Risk Factors for Complicated Grief

**Nature of death**:
- Sudden/unexpected
- Violent or traumatic
- Suicide
- Preventable (perceived)
- Child loss
- Multiple losses

**Relationship factors**:
- Ambivalent relationship
- Highly dependent relationship
- Conflicted relationship
- Unfinished business

**Personal factors**:
- History of depression/anxiety
- Previous losses
- Insecure attachment style
- Limited coping skills
- Low social support
- Prior trauma

**Circumstances**:
- Isolation
- Financial strain
- Secondary losses
- Other concurrent stressors

## Evidence-Based Treatments

### Complicated Grief Treatment (CGT - Shear)

**Components**:
1. **Understanding grief**: Psychoeducation about normal vs. complicated grief
2. **Processing the death**: Revisiting story, imaginal revisiting
3. **Restoring life**: Goals, planning, behavioral activation
4. **Strengthening continuing bond**: Imaginal conversations

**Key Techniques**:
- Imaginal revisiting of death story
- In vivo exposure to avoided situations
- Imaginal conversations with deceased
- Memory work and finding meaning
- Goal setting and activation

### Meaning Reconstruction (Neimeyer)

**Core idea**: Grief involves meaning-making—constructing significance around the loss.

**Techniques**:
- Narrative retelling
- Benefit-finding (post-traumatic growth)
- Identity reconstruction
- Continuing bonds work
- Meaning-oriented questions

### Interpersonal Therapy for Grief

Focus on:
- Role transitions
- Interpersonal disputes
- Interpersonal deficits
- Building/strengthening support

## Therapeutic Interventions

### Early Grief (Acute Phase)

**Focus**: Stabilization and support

**Interventions**:
- Validate and normalize grief responses
- Psychoeducation about grief
- Encourage self-care basics
- Help mobilize support system
- Assess for complicated grief risk
- Provide practical guidance

**What to avoid**:
- Rushing the process
- Platitudes ("They're in a better place")
- Comparisons ("At least you still have...")
- Problem-solving before processing

### Middle Phase

**Focus**: Processing and integration

**Interventions**:
- Tell and retell the story
- Explore relationship with deceased
- Work with avoided material
- Address guilt and anger
- Process secondary losses
- Support identity reconstruction

### Later Phase

**Focus**: Meaning and reinvestment

**Interventions**:
- Meaning-making work
- Continuing bonds development
- Future orientation
- Reinvesting in life
- Addressing avoidance of new relationships
- Rituals and commemoration

## Specific Loss Contexts

### Sudden/Traumatic Death

**Additional challenges**:
- No preparation
- Trauma symptoms overlay grief
- "What ifs" and guilt
- Shattering of assumptions
- Legal/investigative processes

**Approach**:
- Address trauma before/alongside grief
- Allow more time
- Stabilization emphasis
- Meaning-making particularly important

### Suicide Loss

**Unique features**:
- Profound "why" questions
- Intense guilt
- Stigma and isolation
- Anger at deceased
- Worry about others
- Secondary trauma from discovery

**Approach**:
- Psychoeducation about suicide (reduce self-blame)
- Support groups specifically for suicide loss
- Address specific guilt narratives
- Work with anger at deceased
- Community building with others bereaved by suicide

### Anticipatory Grief

**When death is expected**:
- Grief begins before death
- Can coexist with hope
- Opportunity for completion
- Caregiver burden
- Role strain

**Approach**:
- Validate pre-death grief
- Support presence with dying person
- Encourage conversations and closure
- Self-care for caregivers
- Prepare for post-death adjustment

### Disenfranchised Grief

**When grief is not socially acknowledged**:
- Non-traditional relationships
- Stigmatized losses
- Secret relationships
- Pet loss
- Losses not seen as significant

**Approach**:
- Validate the relationship and loss
- Provide permission to grieve
- Create mourning rituals
- Connect with others who understand

## Practical Techniques

### Telling the Story
- Narrative exposure to the loss
- Start to finish, with emotions
- What happened before, during, after
- Repeated telling can integrate

### Empty Chair
- Conversation with deceased
- Say what was unsaid
- Receive imagined response
- Work through unfinished business

### Letter Writing
- Letters to deceased (not sent)
- Letters from deceased (imagined)
- Goodbye letters
- Gratitude letters

### Rituals and Commemoration
- Anniversary observances
- Creating memorials
- Continuing traditions
- New rituals

### Continuing Bonds Exercises
- What would they say?
- Carrying their values forward
- Speaking to their photo
- Visiting meaningful places

### Memory Work
- Photo review
- Life story construction
- Collecting memories from others
- Legacy projects

## What Helps and What Doesn't

### Helpful
- "I'm so sorry for your loss"
- "I don't know what to say, but I'm here"
- "Would you like to talk about [person]?"
- "What do you need right now?"
- Specific offers of help
- Mentioning deceased by name
- Following their lead
- Sitting with silence
- Physical presence

### Unhelpful
- "They're in a better place"
- "I know how you feel"
- "At least they didn't suffer"
- "It was God's will"
- "You need to move on"
- "They wouldn't want you to be sad"
- "Be strong"
- Avoiding the topic
- Comparing losses
- Giving unsolicited advice
- Setting timelines

## Self-Care During Grief

### Physical
- Sleep (grief is exhausting)
- Eat (even without appetite)
- Hydrate
- Gentle movement
- Medical attention if needed

### Emotional
- Feel what you feel
- Don't judge your grief
- Express emotions safely
- Limit alcohol

### Social
- Accept help
- Tell people what you need
- Set boundaries on unsupportive people
- Connect with others who understand

### Practical
- Lower expectations
- One day at a time
- Postpone major decisions
- Keep routines where possible

### Meaning
- Rituals and commemoration
- Spiritual/religious practices
- Connection to community
- Honor the relationship

## Grief in Special Populations

### Children
- Developmental understanding differs
- May need repeated explanations
- Grief expressed through play/behavior
- Need reassurance of own safety
- Concrete language (avoid euphemisms)
- Include them appropriately

### Adolescents
- May grieve privately
- Peer support important
- Identity development impacted
- May act out or withdraw
- Need autonomy AND support

### Older Adults
- Multiple losses common
- Own mortality salient
- May minimize needs
- Physical health impacts
- Social isolation risk

---

## When to Refer

**Consider referral/higher care when**:
- Suicidal ideation
- Prolonged grief disorder criteria met
- Substance use as coping
- Psychotic symptoms
- Inability to function in daily life
- Severe depression
- Trauma symptoms predominating
- No improvement over time

---

*Grief is not a problem to be solved but a process to be accompanied. Healing doesn't mean forgetting—it means integrating the loss into a life that continues to have meaning.*
